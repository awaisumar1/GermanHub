# Vision — GermanHub

## The Core Idea

**Learning a language should feel like exploring a world, not completing a workbook.**

GermanHub is a knowledge graph for the German language. You don't progress through levels. You don't unlock chapters. You open a map, pick something that interests you, and start exploring. One concept leads to another. Connections reveal themselves. Understanding emerges from curiosity, not obligation.

## Exploration, Not Studying

Traditional language learning apps are built around a studying model:

| Studying Model | Exploration Model |
|---|---|
| Linear progression (Chapter 1 → 2 → 3) | Non-linear navigation (follow your curiosity) |
| Content is locked until prerequisites are met | Everything is accessible from the start |
| Knowledge is isolated into lessons | Knowledge is connected into a graph |
| Progress is measured by completion | Progress is measured by territory explored |
| You follow the app's path | You follow your own path |

GermanHub follows the exploration model entirely.

## Inspiration

The experience should remind users of:

- **Wikipedia** — Click a link, discover something new, follow another link. Learning happens through connection-following.
- **Notion / Obsidian** — Information is structured as interconnected nodes. You build your own understanding by navigating relationships.
- **Interactive Knowledge Graphs** — Visual maps of how ideas relate. Zooming in reveals detail; zooming out reveals structure.

It should *not* remind users of:

- **Duolingo** — Gamified drills with hearts, streaks, and XP. Learning as a game to be beaten.
- **Textbook chapter sequences** — Rigid ordering decided by an author. One path for everyone.
- **Flashcard apps** — Memorization through repetition without context or connection.

## What Makes This Different

### 1. The Graph Is the Product

Most language apps have a database behind a UI. GermanHub's graph *is* the UI. You can see how `weil` connects to `denn`, how both connect to "Expressing Reasons," and how that concept sits within the broader landscape of German grammar. The structure of knowledge is visible, not hidden.

### 2. Every Node Is Real

There are no grey locked icons. Every node in the graph is a real destination. If it hasn't been fully built yet, it shows a "Coming Soon" page — but it exists in the graph, it has connections, and it's part of the map. The world feels complete even as it grows.

### 3. No Linear Path

There is no "correct" order. A user who starts with `deshalb` and discovers `weil` through its connections has a valid learning path. The graph supports infinite entry points and infinite paths through the same material.

### 4. Context Over Isolation

A word page doesn't just show a translation and example sentence. It shows where the word sits in the graph — what concept it belongs to, what words it relates to, what themes it appears in. Understanding comes from seeing the neighborhood, not just the node.

### 5. Designed for Curiosity

Every page ends with "Continue Exploring." Breadcrumbs show where you've been. Recent explorations are remembered. The entire UX is designed to reward curiosity and make the next click irresistible.

## The Question This Slice Answers

> Does exploring a knowledge graph of German feel magical?

Not "is the app complete." Not "does it scale." Not "is the content comprehensive."

Just: **does this feel like a better way to learn?**

If the answer is yes, everything else follows.
